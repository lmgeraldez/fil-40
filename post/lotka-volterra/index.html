We use a lot of tools to make sense of the world around us. Even without the advent of mathematics, people have been leveraging problem solving techniques, knowingly or unknowingly in one form or another, to provide solutions to problems or rationalizations to phenomena even as far back with ancient civilizations.

For instance, our parents may have advised us not to shower in the rain as they have associated this with sickness. The patterns that they have seen on people getting sick when soaked in rainwater led to the conception of a model without the need to give explanation why it happens. It just does. This simple exposition of fact becomes a model from which a solution emerges.

Of course, such simplistic model can be prone to inaccuracy; people may not get sick when soaked in the rain or people may get sick but not directly because of the rain. Mathematics provides a way of creating models systematically from which we can quantify a model’s accuracy.
